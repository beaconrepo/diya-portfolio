AI-Mediated Computational Analysis of Bispecific RNA-Based Aptamers Transferrin Receptor of Blood Brain Barrier and EpCAM in Glioblastoma
Diya Kamalabharathy, Poolesville High School, Poolesville, MD

Glioblastoma Multiforme (GBM) is an aggressive and highly malignant type of brain cancer that forms in the brain's glial cells. Targeting the glioblastoma cells is challenging due to the blood-brain barrier (BBB). This highly selective membran-e separates brain blood vessels from the surrounding environment and is essential in regulating substances in the brain‚Äôs capillaries. Transferrin Receptors (TfR) have shown promising results in mediating drug transport across the BBB. Aptamers are nucleic acids modified to possess specific characteristics such as higher binding affinity and specificity. This research aims to design bispecific aptamers that can cross the BBB using TfR and later bind to the overexpressed EpCAM receptor on glioblastoma cells. I hypothesize that drug-bound aptamers can be used as transporting agents that first bind to the TfR, mimicking transferrin protein, and subsequently transport the drug and aptamer across the BBB. This work uses AI and computational simulation techniques to decipher the binding interactions between the TfR and the respective aptamers. Initially, to find the binding site, I first predicted the structure of the transferrin receptors by modeling them using the AlphaFold 3. In the next step, I elucidated the structure of four potential aptamers utilizing the DNAfold and FARFAR2 software. The 3D-modeled aptamers were finally docked on the TfR to understand the binding interactions using the HDOCK2.0 software. The molecular docking results and the aptamer binding site are further validated using a deep cleaning-based method called ScanNet. Finally, I analyzed and found the best binding site and mapped the bonds using the PLIP Observer. I also examined the binding energy of each aptamer to the transferrin receptor using PDA- Pred. I used three parameters to select the aptamers: visual inspection of TfR-aptamer complexes, number of interactions, and binding affinity. The results depicted that aptamer hmBS04 made the most substantial interaction and could be used for the TfR binding. The current results will pave the way for designing bispecific aptamers to cross the blood-brain barrier and target EpCAM-positive cancer cells, leading to targeted glioblastoma therapy.
Keywords: Glioblastoma, Transferrin Receptor (TfR), Aptamer, Deep Learning, AlphaFold 3

INVESTIGATING THE EFFECT OF OVEREXPRESSION OF RHO1 ON CLIMBING SPEED AND ENDURANCE IN DROSOPHILA MELANOGASTER

Diya Kamalabharathy1, Evangeline Chen2, Kenny Nguyen3, Dr. Jeff Leips4
1Poolesville High School, 17501 W Willard Rd, Poolesville, MD 20837
2University of Maryland, College Park, MD, 20742
3Broadneck High School, 1265 Green Holly Dr, Annapolis, MD 21409
4Department of Biological Sciences, University of Maryland, Baltimore County, 1000 Hilltop Circle, Baltimore, MD, 21250

The Rho1 gene has been identified through a genome-wide analysis as a candidate gene affecting physical performance in Drosophila melanogaster. Rho1 is an evolutionarily conserved gene that encodes for proteins in the family of GTPases, which play a key role in the regulation of actin dynamics and wound healing, and therefore potentially impact physical performance. We studied the effect of the Rho1 gene on climbing speed and endurance by manipulating Rho1 expression in the muscle tissue of the fly by utilizing the GAL4-UAS system. Virgin females of the muscle-GAL4 line were crossed with males of the UAS overexpressed Rho1 responder line and males of a control line. We measured the climbing speed and endurance of the offspring of these crosses. This experiment aims to determine whether Rho1 overexpression in muscle tissue influences locomotor performance, including speed and endurance. We hypothesize that an overexpression of the Rho1 gene within flies will have a statistically significant influence on their speed and endurance compared to the control line. The results of our experiment will provide insight into the significance of the Rho1 gene in the physical ability of Drosophila melanogaster, which may suggest the role the human orthologs of Rho1 play in physical performance in humans. This could help enhance personalized healthcare as it relates to physical decline and conditions such as myopathy and muscular dystrophy. If the Rho1 gene is found to affect physical performance in Drosophila melanogaster, further research is needed to determine the underlying mechanisms behind this effect. Future research should investigate the gene‚Äôs effect on other tissues and age groups, given that Rho1 has been characterized as an age-specific gene in a genome-wide analysis of Drosophila melanogaster.
Support for this research was provided by the Department of Biological Sciences at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County.
